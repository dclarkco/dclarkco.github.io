1 | Learning

Modern sport predictions are tough to pull off. Despite there being an immense swath of data being collected, analyzed, and disseminated each and every game, the intricate nuances of player performance over time makes it nearly impossible to predict winners consistently. FiveThirtyEight’s new “Raptor” rating system tries to bridge that gap, and has so far proved an effective mechanism for predicting and analyzing the latest season of NBA basketball. Given these advanced statistics, I want to see which players have the most impact on the court, as well as which teams have the most efficient rosters, to take a peek into the potential future that the playoffs hold.

2 | Winnowing

The dataset being used for this analysis is the 2021 FiveThirtyEight “RAPTOR” set from The Best NBA Players, According To RAPTOR article. This dataset has a few notable new analytics that help us understand NBA basketball. The details of these calculations are explained here, but a short summary is helpful for the purposes of understanding what is being shown in the visualizations.

There are two major variables that I use: RAPTOR Box and RAPTOR On/Off. RAPTOR measures the amount of points contributed, either offensive or defensive, per 100 offensive possessions while they are on the court. It assumes that a team’s total rating is the sum of all it’s players, with more weight being awarded to players that play more often. RAPTOR itself is the combination of RAPTOR box and RAPTOR on/off. RAPTOR box is a net player rating, isolated from the influence of other players. On/Off weighs a player’s score by comparing how their team performs when the player is either on or off the court. Many other factors contribute to a RAPTOR score, such as spacing, shots, rebounds, and the majority of the other datapoints being collected in a game. Many of these factors were consciously weighted higher or lower, and as such, it must be assumed that the data has a bias in short-term and non-standard outliers. In this regard, however, RAPTOR seems to perform better than antiquated analyses such as RAPM, given it’s leaning on historical trends as well as current data.

Overall, RAPTOR is a good metric of performance for players, but it cannot possibly encompass all types of skill and performance, so some players are surely better or worse than their statistics suggest.

3 | Discover

For this analysis, I’ll focus on two tasks:

  1. Which players are the most impactful?

Understanding which players are the most effective on the court isn’t exactly rocket science, but it serves as a good comparison between the best statistical players, and more average players. To do this, every active player (We’ll define this as >500 total possessions) must be charted according to RAPTOR ratings. This will be step one in the process.

  1. Which teams have the most balanced and efficient rosters?

Then, it is important to understand which teams are doing more with less, ie; signing more average or above average players to distribute the responsibility of play more evenly. Some teams load their roster with middle grade talent, and others focus on extreme impact players with a decent, if not insignificant bench. To do this, I will look at the median RAPTOR score per team. A team with many good players will likely have a good RAPTOR median, opposed to a team with more variety in skill that may suffer in this regard. This will be step 2, and I suspect the better corps will be a mix of the two team models outlined above.

4 | Design

To design these visualizations, I imagined that a scatter plot would be best, as it can effectively and easily communicate a 2 dimensional analysis, showing who is under and who is overperforming.

One issue with such a largely populated scatterplot is the opacity of the central data population, as the majority of players are not very far from each other, statistically. I had considered using a logarithmic axis to spread the players out more, but it did not seem to help much in the legibility of the core group, and suppressed true outliers, such as Nikola Jokic. Limiting the population to impactful players (>500 possessions) helps reduce the busy-ness of the visualization, while negligibly impacting the output. It cuts out the strange +-50 rated outliers that only played a few minutes. This is not always useful, and the filter will be assessed per visualization. Sometimes it's fun to see the outliers, even if they don’t have a bearing on the real trends

5 | Implement

So, into Tableau goes the data. I did some filtering and aggregation to get everything together, including a custom color scheme to use wherein each team would be represented by their primary, or secondary color. One odd finding in this process is that a multitude of teams share colors, Midnight Blue is exactly the same shade as Naval Blue, and the Jazz share both their primary and secondary colors with other teams. However, there should be enough differentiation to make useful contrast and allow for picking out the stats of your favorite teams. Alas, here are the three charts:

I executed the scatter plot idea I prototyped earlier, albeit with colors and less perfect axes. Overlap was an issue, but I chose to only show labels for players that are far enough outside the peloton of mediocrity to be distinguishable. Thus, the top players and teams are easy to pick out amidst the mess, and some real trends can be observed.

6 | Deploy

Which players are the most impactful?

Given the caveats of using this data, and how inherently team weighted it can be, lots of good players get caught in the mush because their teams are likewise efficient. Key players, however, that go above and beyond their team efficiency stick out. Nikola Jokic, Steph Curry, James Harden, Damian Lillard, Luka Doncic, and Lebron James are the most prominent, but surprise players such as Joel Embid and Mike Conley stand out as being particularly efficient, when considering their play on both sides of the court.

Which teams have the most balanced and efficient rosters?

Unsurprisingly, the playoff-bound teams dominate Defensive and Offensive ratings, but the underdogs are not far behind. The Bucks are prominent, which goes against their current record as third in the division. Memphis, sitting at 8th, is surprisingly efficient, and I’d wager their team as a whole is underrated given the statistics. Brooklyn seems lower than expected. Otherwise, Good teams like Denver, New Orleans, Utah, Philly, and the Clippers are well represented.

7 | Iterate

Having pored over this dataset, I think combining the team stats with a dedicated RAPTOR team rating, as well as standings, and other metadata such as points scored, free throws, and three pointers would allow for more nuanced peering into meaningful trends. Granted, RAPTOR should take these stats into consideration, I think seeing where and how teams are attributed these higher or lower ratings would be especially useful.

Also, having these stats for players would help isolate players that score or dominate in ways not discernible through a single statistic. Every player contributes differently to their team, and being able to filter through those mechanisms would be helpful. A 3 dimensional visualization would be very interesting in exploring these trends.

8 | Reflect

As described above, this more or less tells me that good teams are good, but for a variety of reasons and players. Some teams certainly do have more middle ground players, while others have standout stars, but the best teams contribute fairly evenly to get wins, with team play such as passing, picking, and rebounds being a very important but under-represented way to attain wins. This also shows that NBA analysis is more than a single statistic, no matter how advanced the analysis of said statistic is, no single player or team can be completely described by one single number.

To design such a visualization, many choices must be made, possibly introducing bias and irregularities in the data. I think the statistics chosen are more fit for analyzing the key differences between players and teams, but a much more nuanced and specific visualization could do so. For the purposes of generalizing a trend in the league, a large population scatter-plot works fine, but it is hard to scratch beneath the surface and truly understand why there is such diversity in skill and scoring. For that, a deeper and more explored data story is needed. Perhaps some more stylistic and focused sets of visualizations would work better. NBA statistics have been and will continue to be a popular source for visualization journalism partly because of this.
