To condition the data for this visualiation, I did a few things. First of all, I dropped NA values to clean up the dataset a bit. Then, I made a new column called 'weekday' that describes each event's weekday of occurence. Also, I changed the datatypes for 'date' and 'time' to be datetime objects, so that pandas and altair can interpret them better. Last, I made two new dataframes, one for arrests only, and another for driving arrests. For this visualization, I only used the former dataframe. As for statistics, I calculated the overall percentages of stops that are arrests (35%), as well as the percentage of those that are vehicular arrests (62%). Likewise, I calculated the amount of arrests made per each weekday; Sunday: 109, Monday: 111, Tuesday: 136, Wednesday: 125, Thursday: 165, Friday: 192, Saturday: 147.

I used Altair to visualize the data, which shows the arrests per time and weekday, split into vehicular and pedestrian groups.

Step 3 | Analysis


I think this data is too limited in scope to take much meaning out of it, as it is limited to one month in 2012. That said, there are some interesting trends in the data; pedestrian arrests are more frequent and later into the night on weekends, and vehicular arrests are less frequent during the day on weekends. Also, I think the lunch break taken by officers is apparent in the data, as there is a strange gap in arrests during lunch hours. Or, maybe people commit less crimes after lunch. It also seems that morning people commit less crimes, as there is a distinct lack of data from about 5am to 8am on most days. Maybe its too early for the police and perpetrators to both be active... Otherwise, people seem the most rowdy near the end of the workweek, where there is a significant uptick in arrests compared to sundays, mondays, and tuesdays. Then again, this data is small in scope and this may just be an anomaly. A question for the future: Are workday arrests more common in downtown areas? Is there a more patterned weekly or diurnal periodic fluctuation in arrests than can be seen in this data? To determine this, an animated map of arrests over long periods of time, aggregated into a weekly scope would be needed. Altair is not the tool for that, unfortunately.
